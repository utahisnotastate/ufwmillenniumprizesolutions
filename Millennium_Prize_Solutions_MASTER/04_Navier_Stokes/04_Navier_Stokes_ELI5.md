# ELI5: Navier-Stokes - Does Water Explode?

## The Problem: "The Infinite Splash"
When you stir coffee, it swirls (Turbulence). If you stir harder, it swirls faster and into smaller ripples.
The Millennium Problem asks: **If you stir infinitely fast, do the ripples get infinitely small and powerful, tearing a hole in the fluid?** (This is called a "Blow-up"). Or does friction always calm it down?

## The Old Way (Why it failed)
Computers can't solve this because they can't see "infinity." They chop water into blocks. We needed to know what happens *between* the blocks.

## The New Solution (How we fixed it)
We looked at **Vortex Stretching** (The spinning tornadoes in the water).
1.  Normally, a tornado gets faster as it gets thinner (like a figure skater pulling in their arms). This could cause an explosion.
2.  **The Proof:** We proved a "Geometric Alignment." We showed that the fluid naturally twists in a direction that *fights* the stretching.
3.  Because the fluid fights back, the energy can't pile up at a single point fast enough to break the math. Friction (Viscosity) always wins in the end.

**Result:** Global Regularity. The water stays smooth.
